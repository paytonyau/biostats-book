# Dedication and Acknowledgments

## Dedication

*For every student who was told that statistics was not for them.*

*And for the residents of Broad Street, Soho — whose deaths, recorded in a ledger in 1854, continue to teach the world how to save lives.*

---

## Acknowledgments

This book would not exist without the intellectual generosity of the open-source community and the public health tradition from which it draws.

**Dr John Snow (1813–1858)**, whose meticulous fieldwork in the 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak established the foundational logic of epidemiological investigation — combining spatial mapping, population data, and qualitative interview to reach a conclusion that contradicted the prevailing medical consensus — remains the animating spirit of every chapter in this text. He did not have software. He had discipline, curiosity, and the courage to walk into a brewery and ask questions.

**Michael Friendly**, whose `HistData` R package preserves Snow's original dataset in a form accessible to every student with a laptop, has done a quiet but significant service to epidemiological education.

**The University of the People**, for its commitment to making higher education accessible to students regardless of geography or financial circumstance, and for the HS 4510 Biostatistics curriculum that gave this book its structure.

**The students of HS 4510**, whose questions — the ones that revealed which explanations were insufficiently clear and which examples failed to connect — shaped every revision of every chapter. A textbook is a dialogue, not a monologue.

**The developers of PSPP and R**, for building and maintaining the free, open-source tools without which health practitioners in low-resource settings could not perform the analyses this book teaches.
