# Foreword

I did not write this book because biostatistics needed another textbook.

I wrote it because I kept watching students — capable, motivated, genuinely committed to improving health outcomes — freeze the moment a formula appeared on the page. Not because they lacked intelligence. Because no one had yet shown them that the formula was not the point. The *question* was the point. The formula was simply the tool we happened to use to answer it.

This book began as a set of notes for **HS 4510 Biostatistics** at the **University of the People** — a course taken by students pursuing the Bachelor of Health Science and the Certificate in Epidemiology. The University of the People is unusual in higher education: it is tuition-free, fully accredited, and serves students in over 200 countries, many of whom are working practitioners — nurses, community health workers, clinic administrators — studying alongside full-time employment and family responsibilities. When I began preparing to teach this course, I looked at the available materials and found textbooks written for research statisticians, freely available resources that assumed mathematical backgrounds my students did not have, and a gap where a practical, applied, readable guide should have been.

What I hope you are now holding — in print or on screen — is an attempt to fill that gap.

## Who This Book Was Written For

Although this book grew from a single course at a single institution, I have deliberately written it to be useful well beyond that context. Whether you are reading this as:

- a **BHS student** completing HS 4510 as a required unit in your degree programme,
- a student on the **Certificate in Epidemiology** who needs to read and produce outbreak analyses with confidence,
- a **health science undergraduate** at any institution seeking a more applied alternative to your assigned textbook, or
- a **self-directed learner** in public health, nursing, or a clinical field who never formally studied statistics but knows you need to,

— this book was written with you in mind.

The University of the People connection explains the book's origin; it does not limit its intended readership. Biostatistics is the common language of evidence-based health practice. It belongs to everyone working to improve population health, regardless of institution, country, or whether they are currently enrolled in a formal course at all.

## Why This Particular Dataset

When I chose the dataset for this book, I had one firm requirement: it had to be real.

Not a simulated dataset constructed to make the numbers come out neatly. Not an anonymous spreadsheet of made-up patients. A genuine historical investigation with genuine stakes.

Dr John Snow's record of the 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak met that requirement completely. It is one of the founding documents of modern epidemiology. It is small enough to be comprehensible to a first-year student. And it is historically documented in enough detail that we can trace exactly how Snow moved from data collection to analysis to action — and what happened when city authorities did and did not act on his conclusions.

More than 600 people died in ten days in a few streets of Soho, London. By working through this dataset, chapter by chapter, you are not practising on a toy problem. You are learning on the kind of problem that epidemiology exists to solve.

## A Word on Preparation and Timing

If you are reading this in preparation for teaching this course yourself, or as supplementary material before your class begins, you are exactly the reader I had in mind when I structured this book the way I did. Each chapter maps to one week of a standard nine-week unit. The chapter sequence is deliberately cumulative: data types (Chapter 1) must precede descriptive statistics (Chapter 2), which must precede inference (Chapters 3–5), and so on. If you are using this book alongside a different course structure, the appendices — particularly the Data Dictionary and the Glossary — are designed to support non-linear reading.

For students: you do not need to have strong prior mathematical training. You need to be willing to think carefully, work through the exercises honestly, and resist the temptation to search for the answer before attempting the question yourself. The Test Your Knowledge sections at the end of each lab are designed specifically for self-assessment — attempt them without the solution visible, then open the dropdown and compare your reasoning, not just your final number.

## Gratitude

This book would not exist without the students who took the time to tell me, clearly and specifically, what was not working. Not the students who quietly withdrew. The ones who wrote in their discussion posts that the p-value definition they had been given made no sense to them, who asked in open forums why the formula for standard deviation had *n−1* in the denominator instead of *n*, who wanted to know what any of this had to do with the work they were actually trying to do in clinics and community health programmes.

Those questions built this book.

Dr John Snow's 1854 fieldwork is the other foundation. He did not have statistical software. He had a notebook, a map, and the discipline to treat observation as evidence rather than as confirmation of what he already believed. That combination — rigour without assumption — remains the right model for evidence-based practice in health science.

I hope this book serves you well, wherever you are and whatever brought you to it.
