# Foreword

In 1948, a small city in Massachusetts made medical history — not by developing a new drug or performing a landmark surgery, but by simply deciding to watch.

The Framingham Heart Study enrolled 5,209 men and women from Framingham, Massachusetts, and committed to following them for the rest of their lives. The researchers did not know at the outset what they would find. They took blood pressure, drew blood for cholesterol, asked about smoking habits, recorded weight and height, noted who developed chest pain, who had a heart attack, who had a stroke, who died. Then they waited. Then they counted.

What they discovered over the next three generations of participants transformed medicine. They proved that high blood pressure damages arteries. They demonstrated that smoking doubles the risk of heart disease. They showed that high cholesterol predicts myocardial infarction. They coined the term *risk factor* — a phrase so embedded in clinical language today that it is hard to believe it needed inventing. Virtually every piece of advice a cardiologist, a GP, or a public health nurse gives about cardiovascular prevention traces its evidence, directly or indirectly, back to Framingham.

The data in this textbook is a teaching subset of that study — 500 participants, de-identified, provided by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute for educational purposes. Every row is a real person who agreed to be measured, examined, and followed so that future generations might live longer. Every variable — systolic blood pressure, total cholesterol, smoking status, BMI — represents a measurement taken at a clinic visit in Framingham between 1948 and 1968.

When a student in this course calculates the mean systolic blood pressure of this cohort, or fits a regression line to see how age predicts cholesterol to visualise survival by smoking status, they are working with the same data that reshaped preventive medicine. The statistical tools in this book are not abstractions. They are the tools that produced the evidence for every hypertension guideline, every smoking cessation recommendation, and every cardiovascular risk calculator in clinical use today.

We hope students approach these pages with that knowledge in mind. Statistics, taught well, is not a requirement to be endured. It is the language in which the story of disease — and the story of its prevention — is told.

*Payton Yau and Suhirthakumar Puvanendran*
